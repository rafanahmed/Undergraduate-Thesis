The **Kelly criterion** gives the fraction of a bankroll that maximizes the expected logarithmic growth rate of wealth in a repeated wagering setting. It was derived by Kelly (1956) and proven optimal by Breiman (1961) — maximizing both the asymptotic growth rate and minimizing the expected time to reach any target wealth level. The criterion assumes that the winning probability $p$ is *known*.

$$
k(p) = \begin{cases} \frac{p\theta - 1}{\theta - 1} & p > 1/\theta \\ 0 & p \leq 1/\theta \end{cases}
$$

Where,

| $k(p)$   | The **Kelly fraction**: optimal proportion of bankroll to wager, as a function of the true winning probability $p$.                |
| -------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $p$      | True probability of placing a winning wager (see [[1.0 - Profitability Condition (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).                         |
| $\theta$ | European (decimal) odds (see [[1.0 - Profitability Condition (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).                                             |

The Kelly fraction $k(p)$ increases linearly in $p$ for $p > 1/\theta$, with slope $\theta/(\theta - 1)$. At $p = 1$, $k(p) = 1$ (bet everything). The criterion is the unique maximizer of the concave expected log growth function (see [[1.1 - Expected Log Growth (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]). The paper's central problem is that $p$ is *unknown*, so $k(p)$ cannot be computed directly — it must be *estimated*. This estimation problem is what motivates the entire modified Kelly framework. The **natural loss function** $l_0$ (see [[2.0 - Natural Loss Function (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]) quantifies the cost of using an estimate $f$ instead of the true $k(p)$.
