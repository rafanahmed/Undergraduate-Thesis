The **Modified Kelly Criterion** $f_0$ is the paper's central result: the Bayes estimator of the optimal betting fraction under the natural loss function $l_0$ and a Beta$(a, b)$ prior on $p$. It has the *identical functional form* as the original Kelly criterion but with the posterior mean $\hat{p}$ replacing the unknown $p$. The resulting fraction is always less than or equal to the standard Kelly fraction $k(x/n)$, providing a theoretically grounded replacement for ad-hoc rules like "half-Kelly."

$$
f_0 = \begin{cases} \frac{\hat{p}\theta - 1}{\theta - 1} & \hat{p} > 1/\theta \\ 0 & \hat{p} \leq 1/\theta \end{cases}
$$

Where,

| $f_0$      | **Modified Kelly fraction**: the Bayes estimator under the natural loss function $l_0$.                                                  |
| ---------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\hat{p}$  | Posterior mean of $p$: $\hat{p} = (x + a)/(n + a + b)$ (see [[3.0 - Beta Posterior Distribution (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).               |
| $\theta$   | European (decimal) odds (see [[1.0 - Profitability Condition (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).                                                   |

$f_0$ is the **convergence point** of the paper's derivation chain: it merges the Kelly criterion's functional form (see [[1.1.1 - Kelly Criterion (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]), the Beta-Binomial posterior (see [[3.0 - Beta Posterior Distribution (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]), and the natural loss function (see [[2.0 - Natural Loss Function (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]) via the expected posterior loss (see [[4.0 - Expected Posterior Loss (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]). The shrinkage from $k(x/n)$ to $f_0$ is *data-adaptive*: strong priors (large $a + b$) produce more shrinkage; large sample sizes (large $n$) reduce shrinkage. As $n \to \infty$, $\hat{p} \to x/n$ and $f_0 \to k(x/n)$ — the modified criterion converges to the original. In the paper's examples, $f_0$ consistently falls near half-Kelly, providing a **theoretical rationale** for that popular heuristic.
