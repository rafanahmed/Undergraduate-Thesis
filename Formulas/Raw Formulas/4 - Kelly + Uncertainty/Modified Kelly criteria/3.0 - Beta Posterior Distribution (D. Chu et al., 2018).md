The **Beta posterior** is the result of combining a Beta$(a, b)$ prior on the winning probability $p$ with a Binomial$(n, p)$ likelihood for the historical data. The **Beta-Binomial conjugacy** ensures the posterior remains in the Beta family, making Bayesian updating a simple parameter update. The posterior mean $\hat{p}$ is a *shrinkage estimator* that pulls the sample proportion $x/n$ toward the prior mean $a/(a+b)$, with the degree of shrinkage controlled by the ratio of prior pseudo-counts to total evidence.

$$
p \mid x \sim \text{Beta}(x + a, \, n - x + b)
$$

Where,

| $p \mid x$    | Posterior distribution of the winning probability, given observed data $x$.                                     |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $x$            | Number of winning historical wagers (observed data).                                                            |
| $n$            | Total number of historical wagers.                                                                              |
| $a$            | First shape parameter of the Beta prior. Interpretable as prior "pseudo-successes."                             |
| $b$            | Second shape parameter of the Beta prior. Interpretable as prior "pseudo-failures."                             |
| $x + a$        | Posterior shape parameter $\alpha_{\text{post}}$: prior pseudo-successes plus observed successes.               |
| $n - x + b$    | Posterior shape parameter $\beta_{\text{post}}$: prior pseudo-failures plus observed failures.                  |

The posterior mean is $\hat{p} = (x + a)/(n + a + b)$, which is the **sufficient statistic** for the Modified Kelly Criterion $f_0$ (see [[4.1 - Modified Kelly Criterion (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]). The prior parameters $(a, b)$ encode the gambler's prior belief: $a = b = 50$ gives $E(p) = 0.5$ and $\text{SD}(p) \approx 0.05$, placing roughly 95% of the prior mass in $(0.4, 0.6)$ — reflecting the *efficient markets intuition* that most systems have winning rates near 50%. As $n \to \infty$, the posterior concentrates at the true $p$, and the posterior mean $\hat{p} \to x/n \to p$, so the modified Kelly converges to the original Kelly. The posterior distribution feeds into the expected posterior loss $G(f)$ (see [[4.0 - Expected Posterior Loss (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).
