The expected log growth is the objective function that the **Kelly criterion** maximizes. Starting from a bankroll $B_0$ and betting fraction $f$, the expected logarithmic return from a single wager captures the *multiplicative compounding* of wealth: because wealth compounds multiplicatively, the logarithm converts the growth process into an additive random walk whose drift should be maximized.

$$
E[\log(B_1(f)/B_0)] = p \log(1 - f + \theta f) + (1 - p) \log(1 - f)
$$

Where,

| $E[\log(B_1(f)/B_0)]$ | Expected log growth rate of bankroll after one wager.                                                            |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $B_0$                  | Initial bankroll before the wager.                                                                               |
| $B_1(f)$               | Bankroll after the wager, a random variable depending on fraction $f$ and the wager outcome.                     |
| $f$                    | Fraction of the bankroll wagered, $f \in [0, 1]$.                                                                |
| $p$                    | True probability of a winning wager (see [[1.0 - Profitability Condition (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).               |
| $\theta$               | European (decimal) odds (see [[1.0 - Profitability Condition (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]).                           |
| $\log(\cdot)$          | Natural logarithm.                                                                                               |

The expected log growth is **concave** in $f$ for fixed $p$ and $\theta$: the first term is concave (composition of concave $\log$ with affine $1 - f + \theta f$) and the second term $\log(1 - f)$ is strictly concave and decreasing in $f$. This concavity guarantees a unique maximum, which is the Kelly fraction $k(p)$ (see [[1.1.1 - Kelly Criterion (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]). The function has two competing effects: the win term rewards larger $f$ (more to gain), while the loss term punishes larger $f$ (more to lose). The Kelly criterion is the exact balance point. The **natural loss function** $l_0$ (see [[2.0 - Natural Loss Function (D. Chu et al., 2018)]]) is defined as the *difference* between this expected log growth evaluated at $k(p)$ and at an alternative $f$.
