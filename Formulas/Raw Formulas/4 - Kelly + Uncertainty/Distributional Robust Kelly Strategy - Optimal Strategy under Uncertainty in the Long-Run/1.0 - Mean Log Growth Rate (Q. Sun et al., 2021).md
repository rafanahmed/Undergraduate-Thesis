The mean log growth rate is the foundational objective of the **Kelly criterion**. In a finite-outcome gambling setting, the gambler repeatedly allocates fractions of wealth across $n$ bets. Returns compound multiplicatively, so the log of wealth over time is a *random walk* with increment $\log(\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b})$. The mean log growth rate captures the expected drift of this random walk — the quantity that Kelly (1956) showed should be maximized for optimal long-run wealth growth.

$$
G_\pi(\mathbf{b}) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\log(\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b})] = \boldsymbol{\pi}^\top \log(\mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{b}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \log(\mathbf{r}_k^\top \mathbf{b})
$$

Where,

| $G_\pi(\mathbf{b})$                    | Mean log growth rate under distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ for bet allocation $\mathbf{b}$.                                     |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\boldsymbol{\pi}$                      | Probability vector $(\pi_1, \dots, \pi_K) \in \mathcal{S}_K$ over the $K$ possible outcomes.                                    |
| $\mathbf{b}$                            | Bet allocation vector in $\mathcal{S}_n$ (the $n$-simplex), where $b_i$ is the fraction of wealth bet on outcome $i$.            |
| $\mathbf{R}$                            | Payoff matrix $\in \mathbb{R}^{n \times K}$ whose columns $\mathbf{r}_1, \dots, \mathbf{r}_K$ are the return vectors.           |
| $\mathbf{r}_k$                          | Return vector for outcome $k$; $r_{k,i} \geq 0$ is the amount won per dollar bet on bet $i$ when outcome $k$ occurs.            |
| $\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b}$           | Wealth growth factor for a single round — the total return as a function of the allocation and the realized return vector.       |
| $\log(\cdot)$                           | Natural logarithm, applied elementwise when acting on a vector.                                                                  |
| $K$                                     | Number of possible outcomes (events).                                                                                            |
| $n$                                     | Number of available bets.                                                                                                        |
| $\mathcal{S}_K$                         | The $K$-dimensional probability simplex: $\{\boldsymbol{\pi} \geq 0 \mid \mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\pi} = 1\}$.               |

$G_\pi(\mathbf{b})$ is the **mean drift** of the log-wealth random walk. Maximizing it is the classic Kelly problem. Concavity holds because $\log(\mathbf{r}_k^\top \mathbf{b})$ is concave in $\mathbf{b}$ (composition of concave $\log$ with affine $\mathbf{r}_k^\top \mathbf{b}$), and a non-negative weighted sum of concave functions is concave. This makes the Kelly problem a tractable **convex optimization** solvable by standard methods.
- The three equivalent forms — expectation, vector inner product, and explicit summation — emphasize that the same object can be viewed as an expected value (probabilistic), a linear algebra operation (computational), or a weighted sum (analytical).
- The assumption $r_n = 1$ a.s. designates the $n$-th bet as a **cash position**: the allocation $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{e}_n$ corresponds to not betting at all.
