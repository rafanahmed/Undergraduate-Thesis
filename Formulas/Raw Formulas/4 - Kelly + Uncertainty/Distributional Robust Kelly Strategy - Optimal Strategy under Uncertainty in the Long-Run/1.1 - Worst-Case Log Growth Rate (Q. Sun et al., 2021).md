In practical applications the true probability distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is never known exactly. Instead, it lies somewhere within an **uncertainty set** $\Xi$ of plausible distributions. The worst-case log growth rate evaluates the mean log growth rate (see [[1.0 - Mean Log Growth Rate (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]) under the *least favorable* distribution in $\Xi$, providing a guarantee that holds regardless of which member of $\Xi$ is the true distribution.

$$
G_\Xi(\mathbf{b}) = \inf_{\boldsymbol{\pi} \in \Xi} G_\pi(\mathbf{b}) = \inf_{\boldsymbol{\pi} \in \Xi} \boldsymbol{\pi}^\top \log(\mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{b})
$$

Where,

| $G_\Xi(\mathbf{b})$                   | Worst-case (smallest) log growth rate over the uncertainty set $\Xi$.                                                            |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\Xi$                                   | Uncertainty set — a prescribed set of plausible probability distributions, $\Xi \subseteq \mathcal{S}_K$.                        |
| $\inf_{\boldsymbol{\pi} \in \Xi}$      | Infimum over all distributions in the uncertainty set.                                                                           |
| $G_\pi(\mathbf{b})$                    | [[1.0 - Mean Log Growth Rate (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]. The expected log growth under a specific distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$.  |
| $\boldsymbol{\pi}$                      | A candidate probability vector in $\Xi$.                                                                                         |
| $\mathbf{R}$                            | Payoff matrix (see [[1.0 - Mean Log Growth Rate (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]).                                                        |
| $\mathbf{b}$                            | Bet allocation vector (see [[1.0 - Mean Log Growth Rate (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]).                                                |

$G_\Xi(\mathbf{b})$ is concave in $\mathbf{b}$ because it is the **infimum of a family of concave functions**. This preserves the convex optimization structure, meaning the robust problem remains tractable in principle. The transition from $G_\pi$ to $G_\Xi$ is the core conceptual step of the paper: replacing optimization under a point estimate with optimization against the worst case within an uncertainty set.
- The shape and size of $\Xi$ control the *conservatism* of the robust strategy. A larger $\Xi$ provides stronger guarantees but yields lower growth rates; a tighter $\Xi$ is less robust but permits higher growth.
