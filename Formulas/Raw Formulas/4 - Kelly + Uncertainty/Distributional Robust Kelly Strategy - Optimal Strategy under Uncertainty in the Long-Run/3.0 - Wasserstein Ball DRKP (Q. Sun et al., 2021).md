The Wasserstein ball uncertainty set uses optimal transport distance to define distributional neighborhoods. Unlike the $f$-divergence set (see [[4.0 - f-Divergence Ball DRKP (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]), the Wasserstein set does *not* require $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ to be absolutely continuous with respect to $\boldsymbol{\pi}_{\text{nom}}$, meaning it can assign probability to outcomes with zero nominal probability — making it effective for hedging against **black swan events**. This formulation (Theorem 5 in the paper) is derived via LP duality on the joint transport-optimization problem from the general DRKP (see [[1.1.1 - Distributional Robust Kelly Problem (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]).

$$
\max_{\mathbf{b},\, \lambda \geq 0} \quad \sum_{j} \pi_{\text{nom},j} \min_i \left(\log(\mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{b})_i + \lambda\, c_{ij}\right) - s\lambda
$$

Where,

| $\mathbf{b}$                              | Bet allocation vector (see [[1.0 - Mean Log Growth Rate (Q. Sun et al., 2021)]]).                                                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| $\lambda$                                  | Dual variable $\in \mathbb{R}_+$, the Lagrange multiplier for the Wasserstein radius constraint.                                                                                    |
| $\pi_{\text{nom},j}$                      | The $j$-th component of the nominal distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}_{\text{nom}}$.                                                                                                    |
| $\min_i(\cdot)$                            | Minimum over outcomes $i = 1, \dots, K$.                                                                                                                                             |
| $\log(\mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{b})_i$      | Log of the wealth growth factor for outcome $i$.                                                                                                                                     |
| $c_{ij}$                                   | Element of the cost matrix $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times K_{\text{nom}}}_+$; the cost of transporting one unit of probability mass from nominal outcome $j$ to outcome $i$.   |
| $s$                                        | Wasserstein radius — the maximum allowed transport cost between $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ and $\boldsymbol{\pi}_{\text{nom}}$.                                                              |

The objective is concave in $(\mathbf{b}, \lambda)$: $\log(\mathbf{R}^\top \mathbf{b})_i + \lambda c_{ij}$ is concave, $\min_i$ preserves concavity, the weighted sum with non-negative $\pi_{\text{nom},j}$ preserves concavity, and $-s\lambda$ is linear. The problem therefore follows **DCP rules**.
- The **Wasserstein distance** (also called earth-mover distance) measures the minimum cost of transporting probability mass, making it *geometry-aware*: it accounts for how far mass must be moved in outcome space, not just how much probability is shifted.
- This connects to the Wasserstein metric used in distributional RL for measuring closeness of value distributions. In the DRKP context, it measures closeness of *probability* distributions over outcomes.
