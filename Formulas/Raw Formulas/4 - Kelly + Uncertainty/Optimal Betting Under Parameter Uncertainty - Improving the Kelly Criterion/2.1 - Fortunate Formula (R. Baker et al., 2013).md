The paper's culminating result: the product of the [[2.0 - Shrinkage Factor Approximation (R. Baker et al., 2013)]] and the Kelly bet $s^*(p) = ((b+1)p - 1)/b$, yielding a self-contained replacement for the Kelly criterion that incorporates parameter uncertainty. Named in reference to Poundstone's "Fortune's Formula" (the Kelly criterion's popular moniker), the **Fortunate Formula** provides an uncertainty-adjusted bet size requiring only the estimated probability, the odds, and the standard error of the estimate.

$$
s^* = \frac{((b+1)p - 1)^3}{b\left\{((b+1)p - 1)^2 + (b+1)^2 \sigma^2\right\}}
$$

Where,

| $s^*$       | **Uncertainty-adjusted optimal bet proportion** — the fraction of the bankroll to wager, accounting for parameter uncertainty.                                  |
| ----------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $p$         | Estimated winning probability (used in place of the unknown true probability).                                                                                  |
| $b$         | Fractional odds: profit per unit wagered on a win. Related to European odds by $b = \theta - 1$.                                                                |
| $\sigma^2$  | Variance of the sampling distribution of the probability estimate.                                                                                              |

The Fortunate Formula is a **cubic-over-quadratic** function of the edge $((b+1)p - 1)$. When $\sigma = 0$, the formula reduces to the standard Kelly criterion: $((b+1)p - 1)/b = s^*(p)$. As $\sigma$ increases, the formula shrinks the bet toward zero, with the rate of shrinkage governed by the ratio of the uncertainty $(b+1)^2 \sigma^2$ to the squared edge $((b+1)p - 1)^2$. The cubic numerator means that the shrinkage is *more aggressive* for small edges than for large ones — a bettor with a marginal edge and modest estimation error will see their bet drastically reduced, while a bettor with a large edge will retain most of their position. This asymmetric sensitivity is a desirable property: marginal edges are precisely where estimation error is most dangerous.
