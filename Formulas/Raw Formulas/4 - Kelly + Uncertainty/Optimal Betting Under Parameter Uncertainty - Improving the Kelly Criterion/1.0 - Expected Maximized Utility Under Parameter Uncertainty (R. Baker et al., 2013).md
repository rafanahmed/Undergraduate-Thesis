When the true winning probability $p$ is unknown and replaced by an estimate $Q$ drawn from a sampling distribution $f(q)$ with mean $p$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the expected maximized utility under **logarithmic utility** becomes an integral over the sampling distribution. This quantity represents the *true* average performance of a Kelly bettor who uses the plug-in estimate $q$ to compute their bet $s^*(q)$, but whose actual wins and losses are governed by the true $p$. It is the central object of study in the paper's shrinkage framework, and it is always *less than* the naive plug-in expected utility because the optimization at $q \neq p$ introduces a systematic performance drag.

$$
E(u^*) = \int_0^1 f(q) \left\{ p \ln(1 + bs^*(q)) + (1 - p) \ln(1 - s^*(q)) \right\} dq
$$

Where,

| $E(u^*)$  | Expected maximized expected utility — the average log-wealth growth rate when the bettor uses their estimated Kelly bet.                                        |
| --------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $f(q)$    | Probability density function of the sampling distribution of the estimated probability $Q$, with mean $p$ and variance $\sigma^2$.                             |
| $p$       | True (unknown) probability of winning the bet.                                                                                                                 |
| $b$       | **Fractional odds** offered by the bookmaker: a winning bet of one unit returns $b$ units of profit. Related to European odds $\theta$ by $b = \theta - 1$.    |
| $s^*(q)$  | Kelly-optimal bet proportion computed at the estimated probability $q$: $s^*(q) = ((b+1)q - 1)/b$.                                                             |
| $\ln$     | Natural logarithm, the basis of logarithmic utility.                                                                                                           |

The integrand is maximized when $s^*(q) = s^*(p)$, i.e., when the estimate equals the true probability. For any non-degenerate sampling distribution ($\sigma^2 > 0$), the integral is **strictly less** than the naive plug-in utility $p \ln(1 + bs^*(p)) + (1 - p)\ln(1 - s^*(p))$. This overestimation of out-of-sample performance by the plug-in approach is the fundamental motivation for bet shrinkage. The gap between $E(u^*)$ and the naive utility is a direct consequence of **Jensen's inequality** applied to the concave log-utility integrand.
