By expanding the expected utility in a Taylor series about $s^*(p)$ and keeping first-order terms in $\sigma^2$, a closed-form approximation for the optimal shrinkage factor is obtained from the [[1.1.1 - First-Order Condition for Optimal Scaling (R. Baker et al., 2013)]]. This is the "back of the envelope" formula that a bettor can compute without numerical integration or distributional assumptions. It depends only on the Kelly bet at the estimated probability, the odds, and the estimation variance.

$$
k^* \approx \frac{s^*(p)^2}{s^*(p)^2 + \left(\frac{b+1}{b}\right)^2 \sigma^2}
$$

Where,

| $k^*$       | Approximate optimal shrinkage factor ‚Äî the scalar multiplier for the Kelly bet.                                                                                 |
| ----------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $s^*(p)$    | Kelly-optimal bet at the estimated probability $p$: $s^*(p) = ((b+1)p - 1)/b$.                                                                                 |
| $b$         | Fractional odds.                                                                                                                                                |
| $\sigma^2$  | Variance of the sampling distribution of the probability estimate $Q$.                                                                                          |

The formula has a **signal-to-noise interpretation**: $k^*$ is the ratio of the squared Kelly bet (the "signal" ‚Äî the magnitude of the intended action) to the signal plus a scaled noise term ($((b+1)/b)^2 \sigma^2$). When $\sigma^2 = 0$, $k^* = 1$ (no shrinkage). As $\sigma^2$ grows, $k^* \to 0$. The half-Kelly strategy ($k = 1/2$) is optimal when $s^*(p)^2 = ((b+1)/b)^2 \sigma^2$, i.e., when the estimation error roughly equals the edge $p - 1/(b+1)$. This provides the first *theoretical justification* for the half-Kelly heuristic, showing it is the correct shrinkage at a specific noise level rather than an arbitrary rule. The approximation is valid for small $\sigma^2$ (first-order Taylor accuracy). Multiplying $k^*$ by the Kelly bet $s^*(p)$ yields the [[2.1 - Fortunate Formula (R. Baker et al., 2013)]].
