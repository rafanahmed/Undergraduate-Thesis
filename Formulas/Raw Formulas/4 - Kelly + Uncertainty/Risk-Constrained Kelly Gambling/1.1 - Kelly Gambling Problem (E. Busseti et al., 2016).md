The Kelly gambling problem is the convex optimization that selects the bet vector $\mathbf{b}$ maximizing the expected growth rate $g(\mathbf{b})$ (see [[1.0 - Expected Growth Rate (E. Busseti et al., 2016)]]) subject to the simplex constraints. It formalizes the principle of **growth-optimal allocation**: the Kelly-optimal bet $\mathbf{b}^*$ causes wealth to grow faster than any other fixed bet almost surely in the long run. The problem is always feasible (the all-cash bet $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{e}_n$ achieves $g = 0$) and has a unique optimum when at least one bet has positive expected excess return.

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\text{maximize} \quad E[\log(\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b})] \\
&\text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{b} = 1, \quad \mathbf{b} \geq 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Where,

| $E[\log(\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b})]$  | [[1.0 - Expected Growth Rate (E. Busseti et al., 2016)]]. The objective to be maximized.                                                              |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| $\mathbf{b}$                            | Decision variable: the bet allocation vector $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.                                                                           |
| $\mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{b} = 1$       | Budget constraint: all wealth must be allocated across the $n$ bets (including cash).                                                                  |
| $\mathbf{b} \geq 0$                    | Long-only constraint: no short-selling or negative bets.                                                                                               |

The Kelly gambling problem is a **convex optimization problem** because the objective is concave and the constraints define a convex set (the probability simplex). A solution $\mathbf{b}^*$ is called a **Kelly-optimal bet**. The trivial bet $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{e}_n$ is Kelly optimal if and only if $E[r_i] \leq 1$ for all $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$ — in which case no bet has positive expected excess return. If any bet has $E[r_i] > 1$, the optimal growth rate is strictly positive. For finite outcome distributions, the problem is solved directly via standard solvers (CVXPY/ECOS). For general infinite distributions, a **projected stochastic gradient method** with step sizes $t_k \to 0$, $\sum t_k = \infty$ converges to the optimum.
