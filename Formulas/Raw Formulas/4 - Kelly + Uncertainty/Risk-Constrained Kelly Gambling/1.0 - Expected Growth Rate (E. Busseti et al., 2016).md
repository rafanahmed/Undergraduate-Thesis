The expected growth rate is the foundational quantity in Kelly gambling. It measures the *drift* of the log-wealth random walk — the average per-period increase in $\log w_t$ — and serves as the objective function for the **Kelly criterion**. Under IID returns, the log-wealth $v_t = \log w_t$ is a sum of IID random variables $\log(\mathbf{r}_s^\top \mathbf{b})$, making the growth rate the natural measure of long-run **multiplicative** compounding performance.

$$
g(\mathbf{b}) = E[\log(\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b})]
$$

Where,

| $g(\mathbf{b})$                      | Expected growth rate of wealth as a function of the bet vector $\mathbf{b}$. The drift of the log-wealth random walk.                                                  |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $E[\cdot]$                            | Expectation over the return distribution.                                                                                                                               |
| $\log$                                | Natural logarithm. Converts the multiplicative wealth process into an additive random walk.                                                                             |
| $\mathbf{r}$                          | Random nonnegative return vector $\mathbf{r} \in \mathbb{R}^n_+$. Each entry $r_i$ is the payoff per unit wagered on bet $i$, with $r_n = 1$ (cash) almost surely.     |
| $\mathbf{b}$                          | Bet allocation vector $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, satisfying $\mathbf{b} \geq 0$ and $\mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{b} = 1$. Entry $b_i$ is the fraction of wealth placed on bet $i$. |
| $\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b}$         | Scalar gross return on the portfolio: the factor by which wealth changes in one period.                                                                                 |

The growth rate $g(\mathbf{b})$ is a **concave function** of $\mathbf{b}$, since $\log$ is concave and $\mathbf{r}^\top \mathbf{b}$ is linear in $\mathbf{b}$. This concavity is what makes Kelly gambling a tractable convex optimization problem. The growth rate is nonnegative at the trivial bet $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{e}_n$ (all cash), where $g(\mathbf{e}_n) = 0$, and strictly positive whenever at least one bet has $E[r_i] > 1$. The log transformation is not merely a mathematical convenience — it is the natural objective for **multiplicative dynamics**, where the law of large numbers applies to sums (of log-returns), not products (of gross returns).
