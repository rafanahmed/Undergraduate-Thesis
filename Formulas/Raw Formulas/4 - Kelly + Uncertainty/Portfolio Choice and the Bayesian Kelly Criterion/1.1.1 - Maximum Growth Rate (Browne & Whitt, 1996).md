The maximum achievable long-run exponential growth rate when the gambler uses the optimal **Kelly fraction** $f^* = 2\theta - 1$ (see [[1.1 - Classical Kelly Fraction (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). This quantity, denoted $\Lambda(\theta)$, has a direct **information-theoretic interpretation**: it equals $\ln 2$ minus the binary entropy $H(\theta)$ of the success probability. The growth rate is thus a measure of the *informational advantage* the gambler has over a fair game — the more predictable the outcome, the higher the growth.

$$
\Lambda(\theta) = \ln 2 + \theta \ln \theta + (1 - \theta)\ln(1-\theta) = \ln 2 - H(\theta)
$$

Where,

| $\Lambda(\theta)$ | Maximum log-growth rate per round under the optimal Kelly fraction.                                              |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\theta$           | True probability of winning ($Z = +1$).                                                                         |
| $\ln 2$            | Growth rate of a deterministic doubling game (the upper bound when $\theta = 1$).                                |
| $H(\theta)$        | Binary entropy: $H(\theta) = -\theta \ln \theta - (1-\theta)\ln(1-\theta)$. Measures the uncertainty of the binary outcome. |

The function $\Lambda(\theta)$ is **convex** in $\theta$, achieving its minimum of $0$ at $\theta = 1/2$ (fair game, maximum entropy) and its maximum of $\ln 2$ at $\theta = 1$ (certain win, zero entropy). This convexity plays a central role in the Bayesian analysis: by Jensen's inequality, the *expected* growth rate under an uncertain $\theta$ exceeds the growth rate at the *mean* of $\theta$. This is the information-theoretic basis for the **financial value of randomization** — having a distribution over $\theta$ (rather than a point mass at $E[\theta]$) can increase expected growth, provided the gambler can condition their bets on the realized $\theta$. The posterior analogue $\Lambda(S_j, j)$ appears in the discrete Bayesian value function (see [[3.1 - Discrete Bayesian Kelly Rule (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]).
