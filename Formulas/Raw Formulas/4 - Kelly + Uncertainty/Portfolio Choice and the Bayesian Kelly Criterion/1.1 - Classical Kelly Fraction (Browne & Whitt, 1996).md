The optimal betting fraction for a gambler facing repeated independent rounds of a binary game with known success probability $\theta > 1/2$. By maximizing the expected log-growth rate $G(f) = \theta \ln(1+f) + (1-\theta)\ln(1-f)$ over the fraction $f$ wagered at each step, the gambler arrives at the **Kelly criterion** — the strategy that maximizes the long-run exponential growth rate of wealth under the **multiplicative wealth dynamic** (see [[1.0 - Multiplicative Wealth Dynamic (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). The optimal fraction equals the *expected value* of the game increment.

$$
f^* = 2\theta - 1 = E[Z]
$$

Where,

| $f^*$    | Optimal Kelly fraction — the proportion of wealth to wager at each step.                                         |
| -------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\theta$ | True probability of winning ($Z = +1$). Must satisfy $\theta > 1/2$ for the game to be favorable.               |
| $E[Z]$   | Expected value of the payoff increment: $E[Z] = \theta \cdot 1 + (1-\theta) \cdot (-1) = 2\theta - 1$.         |

The Kelly fraction has several notable properties. It is **proportional to the edge**: the greater the deviation of $\theta$ from $1/2$, the larger the bet. It is **zero for a fair game** ($\theta = 1/2$) — the Kelly gambler never bets on a coin with no edge. It is **linear in $\theta$** — a property that holds only in the binary case and simplifies much of the subsequent analysis. This linearity is what makes the **certainty equivalence** principle work in the Bayesian extension (see [[3.1 - Discrete Bayesian Kelly Rule (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]): replacing $\theta$ by its posterior mean $E(\theta \mid \text{data})$ in a linear function preserves optimality under logarithmic utility.
