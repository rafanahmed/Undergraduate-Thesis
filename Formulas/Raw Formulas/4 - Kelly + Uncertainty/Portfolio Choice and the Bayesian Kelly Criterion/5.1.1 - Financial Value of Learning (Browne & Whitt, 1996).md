The quantitative measures of the **financial value of information** and the **cost of learning** in the Bayesian investment framework. By comparing the **Bayesian value function** $F^B(x)$ (see [[5.1 - Bayesian Value Function (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]) against the **non-Bayesian baseline** $F(x)$ (see [[2.2 - Continuous-Time Value Function (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]) and the perfect-information benchmark $F^P(x)$, the paper decomposes the total value of a random drift into the portion captured by learning and the portion lost to estimation uncertainty.

$$
\begin{aligned}
F^B(x) - F(x) &= \frac{c}{2\sigma^2}T - \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{c}{\sigma^2}T\right) \\[6pt]
F^P(x) - F^B(x) &= \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{c}{\sigma^2}T\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Where,

| $F^B(x)$                                              | Bayesian value function (see [[5.1 - Bayesian Value Function (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]).                         |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $F(x)$                                                 | Non-Bayesian value function with known drift $\mu$ (see [[2.2 - Continuous-Time Value Function (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). |
| $F^P(x)$                                               | Perfect-information value: $\ln x + T(\mu^2 + c)/(2\sigma^2)$ тАФ the value when $Z$ is known before trading.    |
| $c$                                                    | Prior variance of the unobserved drift $Z$.                                                                      |
| $\sigma^2$                                             | Known variance rate.                                                                                             |
| $T$                                                    | Investment horizon.                                                                                              |
| $F^B(x) - F(x)$                                       | **Bayesian gain over naive**: the benefit of learning the random drift, net of the learning penalty.             |
| $F^P(x) - F^B(x)$                                     | **Cost of learning**: the performance gap between the Bayesian investor and a perfectly informed investor.       |

The first equation shows the **net gain from Bayesian learning** is always positive for $c > 0$: the linear term $cT/(2\sigma^2)$ dominates the logarithmic penalty $\frac{1}{2}\ln(1 + cT/\sigma^2)$ for all $T > 0$. The second equation тАФ the **cost of learning** тАФ quantifies how much the Bayesian investor sacrifices by having to learn $Z$ on the fly rather than knowing it *a priori*. This cost grows only **logarithmically** in $T$, in stark contrast to the **linear** gain from perfect information ($cT/(2\sigma^2)$ from Equation 12). The ratio of cost to potential gain is $\ln(1 + cT/\sigma^2) / (cT/\sigma^2) \to 0$ as $T \to \infty$. This asymptotic vanishing of the cost of learning is the paper's most powerful economic conclusion: in the long run, the Bayesian adaptive investor performs almost as well as the perfectly informed investor.
