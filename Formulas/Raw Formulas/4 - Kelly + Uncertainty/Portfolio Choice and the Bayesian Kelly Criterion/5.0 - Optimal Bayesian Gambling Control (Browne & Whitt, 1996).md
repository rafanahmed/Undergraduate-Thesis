The central result of the paper: the **optimal investment fraction** in continuous time when the drift is unobserved and must be learned from the observation process. This formula is the Bayesian extension of the **continuous-time Kelly fraction** $f^* = \mu/\sigma^2$ (see [[2.1 - Continuous-Time Kelly Fraction (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). The unknown drift $Z$ is replaced by its **posterior mean** $\hat{Z}_t = (cX_t + \mu\sigma^2)/(\sigma^2 + ct)$ (see [[4.0 - Posterior Mean of Random Drift (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]), and the standard Kelly formula is applied as if the posterior mean were the truth. Under logarithmic utility, this **certainty equivalent** policy is provably optimal — the **Separation Principle** holds, and estimation decouples from control.

$$
f_t^* = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\left(\frac{cX_t + \mu\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + ct}\right) = \frac{\hat{Z}_t}{\sigma^2}
$$

Where,

| $f_t^*$                                                 | Optimal proportion of wealth invested in the risky opportunity at time $t$.                                      |
| -------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\sigma^2$                                               | Known variance rate of the risky opportunity.                                                                    |
| $c$                                                      | Prior variance of the unobserved drift $Z$.                                                                      |
| $X_t$                                                    | Observed diffusion process — the cumulative return (see [[4.1 - Filtered Diffusion SDE (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). |
| $\mu$                                                    | Prior mean of $Z$.                                                                                               |
| $\sigma^2 + ct$                                          | Total precision at time $t$: prior precision $\sigma^2$ plus accumulated data precision $ct$.                   |
| $\hat{Z}_t$                                              | Posterior mean of $Z$ given $\mathcal{F}_t^X$ (see [[4.0 - Posterior Mean of Random Drift (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). |

The formula $f_t^* = \hat{Z}_t / \sigma^2$ is structurally identical to the known-drift Kelly rule $f^* = \mu/\sigma^2$ — the only change is that the fixed drift is replaced by its time-varying Bayesian estimate. This **certainty equivalence** is a non-trivial optimality result, not merely a heuristic. The proof proceeds by showing that (1) the filtered process (see [[4.1 - Filtered Diffusion SDE (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]) is a sufficient statistic for the control problem, and (2) the logarithmic objective decouples the optimization from the filtering. The optimal control is **myopic** — it depends only on the current posterior mean, not on the remaining horizon — and **adaptive** — it updates continuously as new observations refine $\hat{Z}_t$. This property holds for *any* prior $G$ on $Z$, not only for the Gaussian case: the general formula is $f_t^* = E[Z \mid \mathcal{F}_t^X] / \sigma^2$. The certainty equivalence principle **fails** for all non-logarithmic utility functions in the HARA family (see [[7.0 - Power Utility Bayesian Control (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]).
