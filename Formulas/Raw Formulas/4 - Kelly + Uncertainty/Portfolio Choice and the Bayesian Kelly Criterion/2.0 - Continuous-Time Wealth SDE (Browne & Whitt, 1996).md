The continuous-time diffusion analogue of the discrete **multiplicative wealth dynamic** (see [[1.0 - Multiplicative Wealth Dynamic (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). As the time step $\Delta \to 0$ and the number of rounds $n \to \infty$ under appropriate scaling, the discrete wealth process converges to a geometric Brownian motion controlled by the investment fraction $f_t$. This SDE is the standard framework for continuous-time portfolio selection, used by Merton (1971) and forming the basis of the paper's Bayesian control problem.

$$
dV_t = f_t V_t \mu \, dt + \sigma f_t V_t \, dW_t
$$

Where,

| $V_t$   | Wealth at continuous time $t$.                                                                                   |
| ------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $f_t$   | Proportion of wealth invested in the risky opportunity at time $t$.                                              |
| $\mu$   | Drift parameter — the expected instantaneous rate of return of the risky opportunity.                            |
| $\sigma$ | Volatility parameter — the diffusion coefficient of the risky opportunity. Assumed known and constant.          |
| $W_t$   | Standard Brownian motion driving the randomness in returns.                                                      |
| $dt$    | Infinitesimal time increment.                                                                                    |
| $dW_t$  | Brownian increment: $dW_t \sim N(0, dt)$.                                                                       |

The SDE has the **geometric form** $dV_t / V_t = f_t(\mu \, dt + \sigma \, dW_t)$, meaning returns are proportional to current wealth — the same multiplicative structure as the discrete case. Under constant control $f_t = f$, the explicit solution is $V_t = V_0 \exp((f\mu - \frac{1}{2}f^2\sigma^2)t + f\sigma W_t)$, which yields the **continuous-time Kelly fraction** $f^* = \mu/\sigma^2$ (see [[2.1 - Continuous-Time Kelly Fraction (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]). When the drift $\mu$ is replaced by an unobserved random variable $Z$, this SDE becomes the object of the Bayesian control problem solved in [[5.0 - Optimal Bayesian Gambling Control (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]].
