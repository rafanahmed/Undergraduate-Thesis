The optimal proportion of wealth to invest in the risky opportunity in continuous time, when the drift $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$ are both known constants. This is the diffusion limit of the discrete **Kelly fraction** $f^* = (2\theta - 1)/\Delta$ (see [[1.1 - Classical Kelly Fraction (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]) under the scaling $\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \mu/\Delta)$ as $\Delta \to 0$. It maximizes the expected logarithmic growth rate of wealth governed by the **continuous-time wealth SDE** (see [[2.0 - Continuous-Time Wealth SDE (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]]).

$$
f^* = \frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}
$$

Where,

| $f^*$    | Optimal continuous-time Kelly fraction — the proportion of wealth allocated to the risky opportunity.            |
| -------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\mu$    | Drift parameter of the risky opportunity.                                                                        |
| $\sigma^2$ | Variance rate (squared volatility) of the risky opportunity.                                                  |

The fraction $f^* = \mu/\sigma^2$ is the **risk-adjusted edge**: the drift per unit variance. It has several key properties. It is **proportional to drift** and **inversely proportional to variance** — more volatile opportunities receive smaller allocations for the same drift. It can exceed $1$ (leveraged investment) or be negative (short selling), reflecting the unconstrained nature of the continuous-time model. Under this optimal control, the growth rate of log-wealth is $\mu^2/(2\sigma^2)$, which is half the squared **Sharpe ratio**. This fraction becomes the template for the Bayesian extension: in [[5.0 - Optimal Bayesian Gambling Control (Browne & Whitt, 1996)]], the unknown $\mu$ is replaced by the posterior mean $\hat{Z}_t$, yielding $f_t^* = \hat{Z}_t / \sigma^2$.
