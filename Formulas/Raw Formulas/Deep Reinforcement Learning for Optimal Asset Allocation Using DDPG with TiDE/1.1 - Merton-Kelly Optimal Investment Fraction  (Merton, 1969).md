Modeling wealth as a ***multiplicative* process** is architecturally essential for long-term capital preservation. 
- In financial environments, investment outcomes compound: 
	- Example: a loss of 10% requires an 11.1% gain to recover, which is a reality that additive models fail to capture. 
- To determine the *magnitude* of the investment action (defined here as the portfolio weight $\omega$):
	- We utilize the **Optimal Investment Fraction**:
$$
\pi^* = \frac{\mu - r_f}{R_a \, \sigma^2}
$$
Where,

| $\pi^*$  | Optimal fraction (weight $\omega$) of capital allocated to the risky asset (e.g., the CRSP index). |
| -------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\mu$    | Expected return of the risky asset.                                                                |
| $r_f$    | Risk-free rate of return.                                                                          |
| $\sigma$ | Standard deviation (volatility) of the risky asset's returns.                                      |
| $R_a$    | Coefficient of constant relative risk aversion (CRRA parameter).                                   |
| $-$      | Subtraction — the numerator $\mu - r_f$ is the *risk premium*.                                     |

This formula, derived using **Itô's Lemma** on the continuous-time wealth process, serves as the primary **bet-sizing mechanism**. 

It calibrates the allocation magnitude by balancing the risk premium $$$\mu - r_f$$against volatility $$\sigma^2$$which is then  scaled by the investor's risk tolerance  $$R_a$$When $R_a = 1$ (which is log-utility denotion), this reduces to the classic **Kelly fraction** (see [[1.0 - Log-Utility Maximization Identity (Kelly, 1956)]]). 

The formula encodes a fundamental trade-off: 
- Higher expected excess return increases the optimal bet, but higher variance decreases it quadratically. 
- This is the mechanism that prevents the agent from over-leveraging in volatile markets and under-investing in calm ones.
