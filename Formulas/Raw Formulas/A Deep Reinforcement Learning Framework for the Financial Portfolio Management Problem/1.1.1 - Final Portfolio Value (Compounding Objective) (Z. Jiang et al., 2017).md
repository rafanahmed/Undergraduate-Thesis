The terminal objective of any portfolio management policy is the maximization of accumulated wealth over a finite trading horizon. 

This formula expresses total wealth as the product of a sequence of per-period growth factors, capturing the compounding nature of investment returns:
$$
p_f = p_0 \prod_{t=1}^{t_f+1} \mathbf{y}_t \cdot \mathbf{w}_{t-1}
$$

Where,

| $p_f$              | Final portfolio value at the end of the trading horizon.                                                             |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $p_0$              | Initial capital at the start of the trading session.                                                                 |
| $\prod$            | Product operator — iterates over all trading periods, multiplying each period's growth factor.                       |
| $t_f$              | Index of the final trading period; the total number of periods in the trading horizon.                               |
| $\mathbf{y}_t$     | Price relative vector at period $t$ (see [[1.0 - Price Relative Vector (Z. Jiang et al., 2017)]]).                         |
| $\mathbf{w}_{t-1}$ | Portfolio weight vector at the beginning of period $t$ (see [[1.1 - Portfolio Value Transition (Z. Jiang et al., 2017)]]). |
| $\cdot$            | Dot product between $\mathbf{y}_t$ and $\mathbf{w}_{t-1}$.                                                           |

This formula defines the **global objective function** for the portfolio management agent in its most direct form: maximize terminal wealth.

The product structure makes explicit that every period's allocation decision feeds into all future periods. For example, a poor allocation early compounds into significant underperformance later. 

In the idealized setting (no transaction costs), this is the quantity the agent seeks to maximize. However, because direct optimization of a product is numerically unstable for gradient methods, the framework converts this into a sum of logarithms (see [[1.1.2 - Logarithmic Rate of Return (Z. Jiang et al., 2017)]] and [[2.1 - Average Logarithmic Accumulated Return (Objective Function) (Z. Jiang et al., 2017)]]). 

Additionally, this idealized form ignores transactional friction; the cost-adjusted version requires the [[1.2.1 - Transaction Remainder Factor (Z. Jiang et al., 2017)]] to be embedded within each period's growth factor.
