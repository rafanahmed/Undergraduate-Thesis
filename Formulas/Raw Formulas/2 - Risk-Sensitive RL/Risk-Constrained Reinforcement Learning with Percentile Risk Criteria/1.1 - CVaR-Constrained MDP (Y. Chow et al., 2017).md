The **CVaR-constrained MDP** formulation separates the performance objective from the risk measure: minimize expected cumulative cost subject to a bound on the CVaR of cumulative constraint cost. This constrained formulation is more natural than unconstrained risk-sensitive objectives in applications where a practitioner has a concrete risk budget $\beta$ (e.g., "the 95th-percentile constraint cost must not exceed $\beta$"). By introducing the auxiliary VaR parameter $\nu$ from the CVaR minimization formula (see [[1.0 - CVaR Minimization Formula (Y. Chow et al., 2017)]]), the CVaR constraint is reformulated as a smooth inequality involving $H_\alpha$, enabling gradient-based optimization.

$$
\min_{\theta, \nu} V^\theta(x_0) \quad \text{subject to} \quad H_\alpha(J^\theta(x_0), \nu) \leq \beta
$$

Where,

| $V^\theta(x_0)$                | Expected cumulative discounted cost under policy $\mu(\cdot \mid x; \theta)$: $V^\theta(x_0) = \mathbb{E}[G^\theta(x_0)]$ where $G^\theta(x_0) = \sum_{k=0}^{T-1} \gamma^k C(x_k, a_k)$. |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\theta$                        | Policy parameter vector, $\theta \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^\kappa$.                                                                                                                   |
| $\nu$                           | Auxiliary VaR parameter, jointly optimized with $\theta$ (see [[1.0 - CVaR Minimization Formula (Y. Chow et al., 2017)]]).                                                                  |
| $x_0$                           | Fixed initial state.                                                                                                                                                                         |
| $H_\alpha(J^\theta(x_0), \nu)$ | CVaR upper bound: $\nu + \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\mathbb{E}[(J^\theta(x_0) - \nu)^+]$. Equals $\text{CVaR}_\alpha(J^\theta(x_0))$ at the optimal $\nu$.                                         |
| $J^\theta(x_0)$                | Cumulative discounted **constraint cost**: $J^\theta(x_0) = \sum_{k=0}^{T-1} \gamma^k D(x_k, a_k)$.                                                                                        |
| $\alpha$                        | Confidence level for CVaR, $\alpha \in (0, 1)$.                                                                                                                                             |
| $\beta$                         | Cost tolerance (risk budget). The CVaR of the constraint cost must not exceed $\beta$.                                                                                                       |
| $\gamma$                        | Discount factor, $\gamma \in (0, 1)$.                                                                                                                                                       |

The formulation uses *two* distinct cost functions: $C(x, a)$ for the primary objective and $D(x, a)$ for the constraint. This dual-cost structure is characteristic of **Constrained MDPs** (Altman, 1999). In many applications $C = D$ (the same cost is both optimized and constrained), but the general formulation permits them to differ. The equivalence between the original CVaR constraint $\text{CVaR}_\alpha(J^\theta(x_0)) \leq \beta$ and the reformulated $H_\alpha$ constraint follows from a feasibility argument: any feasible $\theta$ for the original yields a feasible $(\theta, \nu)$ for the reformulation by setting $\nu = \text{VaR}_\alpha$, and conversely. **Strict feasibility** (Assumption 4 / Slater's condition) â€” the existence of a policy strictly satisfying the constraint â€” is required to guarantee the existence of a local saddle point for the Lagrangian relaxation (see [[2.0 - CVaR Lagrangian Function (Y. Chow et al., 2017)]]).
