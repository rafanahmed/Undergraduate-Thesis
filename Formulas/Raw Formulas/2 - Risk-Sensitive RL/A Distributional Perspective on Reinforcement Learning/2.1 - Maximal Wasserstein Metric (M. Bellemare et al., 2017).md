The maximal Wasserstein metric extends the pointwise Wasserstein distance $d_p$ (see [[2.0 - Wasserstein Metric (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]) from individual distributions to **value distributions** — mappings from the entire state-action space to distributions over returns. It measures the worst-case distributional distance across all state-action pairs, analogous to how the $L_\infty$ norm measures worst-case error across components of a vector.

This metric is the space in which the paper proves its central contraction result: the distributional Bellman operator $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in $\bar{d}_p$ (see [[3.2 - Distributional Bellman Operator (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).

$$
\bar{d}_p(Z_1, Z_2) := \sup_{x, a}\; d_p(Z_1(x, a),\; Z_2(x, a))
$$

Where,

| $\bar{d}_p(Z_1, Z_2)$ | Maximal Wasserstein $p$-distance between two value distributions $Z_1$ and $Z_2$.                                                                              |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $Z_1, Z_2$             | Value distributions in $\mathcal{Z}$ (the space of value distributions with bounded moments). Each maps state-action pairs $(x, a)$ to distributions over returns. |
| $\sup_{x, a}$          | Supremum over all state-action pairs in $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{A}$.                                                                                      |
| $d_p$                   | Wasserstein $p$-distance between scalar distributions (see [[2.0 - Wasserstein Metric (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).                                           |
| $Z_i(x, a)$            | The return distribution at state-action pair $(x, a)$ under value distribution $Z_i$.                                                                           |

The metric $\bar{d}_p$ is confirmed to be a proper metric over value distributions (the triangle inequality follows from applying $d_p$'s triangle inequality pointwise and then taking the supremum). This is necessary for applying Banach's fixed point theorem to establish the unique fixed point $Z^\pi$ of the distributional Bellman operator.

The supremum structure means that convergence in $\bar{d}_p$ implies convergence *uniformly* across all state-action pairs. Combined with $d_p$'s property of controlling all moments, convergence in $\bar{d}_p$ guarantees that the mean, variance, and all higher moments of the iterates $Z_k(x, a)$ converge uniformly to those of $Z^\pi(x, a)$.
