The distributional Bellman operator $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ is the formal operator that implements the distributional Bellman equation (see [[3.0 - Distributional Bellman Equation (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]) as a map on value distributions. It combines three operations: shifting by the random reward $R(x, a)$, scaling by the discount factor $\gamma$, and mixing over next-state return distributions via the transition operator $P^\pi$ (see [[3.1 - Transition Operator (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).

This is the paper's headline theoretical result for policy evaluation: $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in the maximal Wasserstein metric $\bar{d}_p$ (see [[2.1 - Maximal Wasserstein Metric (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]). By Banach's fixed point theorem, it has a unique fixed point — the true value distribution $Z^\pi$.

$$
\mathcal{T}^\pi Z(x, a) \stackrel{D}{:=} R(x, a) + \gamma\,P^\pi Z(x, a)
$$

Where,

| $\mathcal{T}^\pi$      | Distributional Bellman operator for policy $\pi$. Maps a value distribution $Z \in \mathcal{Z}$ to $\mathcal{T}^\pi Z \in \mathcal{Z}$. |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $R(x, a)$              | Random reward at $(x, a)$, treated as a random variable.                                                                                 |
| $\gamma$                | Discount factor, $\gamma \in [0, 1]$.                                                                                                   |
| $P^\pi Z(x, a)$        | [[3.1 - Transition Operator (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]. The next-state return distribution $Z(X', A')$ under $P$ and $\pi$.          |
| $\stackrel{D}{:=}$     | Definitional equality in distribution.                                                                                                   |
| $\mathcal{Z}$          | Space of value distributions with bounded moments.                                                                                       |

The compound distribution $\mathcal{T}^\pi Z$ arises from three *independent* sources of randomness:
- The randomness in the reward $R$
- The randomness in the transition $P^\pi$ (which next state-action pair occurs)
- The next-state value distribution $Z(X', A')$

The **contraction** proof proceeds by applying the Wasserstein metric's properties to each component of the operator. Property (P2) removes the shared reward $R(x, a)$ (shifting by an independent random variable does not increase Wasserstein distance). Property (P1) extracts $\gamma$ as the contraction factor. The transition operator $P^\pi$ contributes at most a factor of 1 (mixing reduces distance). The result is $\bar{d}_p(\mathcal{T}^\pi Z_1, \mathcal{T}^\pi Z_2) \le \gamma\,\bar{d}_p(Z_1, Z_2)$.

The same operator is **not** a contraction in total variation distance, KL divergence, or Kolmogorov distance — the choice of Wasserstein metric is essential, not merely convenient.
