The distributional Bellman equation is the paper's central object. It is the distributional analogue of the classical Bellman equation: where the standard equation relates *expected values* recursively, this equation relates *distributions* recursively. It states that the distribution of the random return $Z$ at any state-action pair $(x, a)$ is equal in law to the distribution obtained by adding the random reward $R(x, a)$ to a $\gamma$-discounted copy of the return distribution at the random next state-action pair $(X', A')$.

The notation $\stackrel{D}{=}$ denotes equality in distribution — the random variable on the left has the same probability law as the expression on the right, though they are not necessarily the same random variable. Three independent sources of randomness interact to form the compound distribution: the reward $R$, the transition to $(X', A')$, and the return $Z(X', A')$ at the successor.

$$
Z(x, a) \stackrel{D}{=} R(x, a) + \gamma\,Z(X', A')
$$

Where,

| $Z(x, a)$      | Random return at state-action pair $(x, a)$. Its expectation is the value function $Q^\pi(x, a)$ (see [[1.0 - Value Function (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]). |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\stackrel{D}{=}$ | Equality in distribution: the left and right sides have the same probability law.                                                                          |
| $R(x, a)$      | Random reward received upon taking action $a$ in state $x$. Treated as a random variable, not a deterministic function.                                       |
| $\gamma$        | Discount factor, $\gamma \in [0, 1]$.                                                                                                                        |
| $Z(X', A')$    | Random return at the next state-action pair. A random distribution because the successor $(X', A')$ is itself random.                                         |
| $X'$            | Next state, drawn from the transition kernel $P(\cdot \mid x, a)$.                                                                                           |
| $A'$            | Next action, drawn from the policy $\pi(\cdot \mid X')$.                                                                                                     |

The distributional Bellman equation is the conceptual foundation from which the paper builds two formal operators: the **transition operator** $P^\pi$ (see [[3.1 - Transition Operator (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]), which handles the next-state mixing, and the **distributional Bellman operator** $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ (see [[3.2 - Distributional Bellman Operator (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]), which combines the reward shift, discount scaling, and transition mixing into a single operator on value distributions.

The equation captures the *intrinsic randomness* of the agent's interactions with its environment — not uncertainty *about* the environment, but randomness *within* it. Even in a deterministic environment, state aliasing from partial observability can produce effective stochasticity in the return distribution. By modelling this distribution explicitly rather than collapsing it to an expectation, the paper argues that approximate RL becomes significantly more stable.
