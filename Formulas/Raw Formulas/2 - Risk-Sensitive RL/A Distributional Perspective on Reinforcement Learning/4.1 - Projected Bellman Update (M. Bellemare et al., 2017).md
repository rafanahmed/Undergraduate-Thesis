The projected Bellman update solves the core algorithmic problem of the categorical approach: the Bellman-updated atoms $\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j = r + \gamma z_j$ generally fall between the fixed support atoms $\{z_i\}$, making the updated distribution incompatible with the parametrization (see [[4.0 - Categorical Distribution Parametrization (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]). The projection $\Phi$ redistributes the probability mass of each updated atom to its two nearest neighbours in the original support, proportionally to their distance.

This projection reduces the distributional Bellman update to a form that can be trained as **multiclass classification**: the projected target is a categorical distribution over the same $N$ atoms as the model output, and the loss is a cross-entropy (see [[4.1.1 - Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).

$$
\left(\Phi\,\hat{\mathcal{T}}\,Z_\theta(x, a)\right)_i = \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} \left[1 - \frac{\left|[\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j]_{V_{\text{MIN}}}^{V_{\text{MAX}}} - z_i\right|}{\Delta z}\right]_0^1 p_j(x', \pi(x'))
$$

Where,

| $\Phi$                                                        | Projection operator that maps the Bellman-updated distribution back onto the fixed support $\{z_i\}$.                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j$                                        | Bellman-updated atom location: $\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j = r + \gamma z_j$, where $r$ is the sampled reward and $z_j$ is the $j$-th atom.                                           |
| $[\cdot]_{V_{\text{MIN}}}^{V_{\text{MAX}}}$                   | Clipping operator that bounds its argument to the range $[V_{\text{MIN}}, V_{\text{MAX}}]$.                                                                                      |
| $z_i$                                                          | The $i$-th atom in the fixed support (see [[4.0 - Categorical Distribution Parametrization (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).                                                       |
| $\Delta z$                                                     | Atom spacing: $(V_{\text{MAX}} - V_{\text{MIN}}) / (N - 1)$ (see [[4.0 - Categorical Distribution Parametrization (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]).                               |
| $[\cdot]_0^1$                                                  | Clipping to $[0, 1]$, ensuring only the two nearest atoms receive probability (the expression is zero for atoms further than $\Delta z$ away).                                  |
| $p_j(x', \pi(x'))$                                            | Probability of atom $z_j$ under the target network's distribution at the next state $x'$ and greedy action $\pi(x') = \arg\max_a \mathbb{E}[Z_\theta(x', a)]$.                 |
| $N$                                                            | Number of atoms.                                                                                                                                                                 |
| $x'$                                                           | Next state from the sampled transition.                                                                                                                                          |

The projection is computable in $O(N)$ time. For each target atom $\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j$, the algorithm computes its fractional index $b_j = (\hat{\mathcal{T}}z_j - V_{\text{MIN}}) / \Delta z$, then distributes the probability $p_j(x', \pi(x'))$ to the floor atom $z_{\lfloor b_j \rfloor}$ and ceiling atom $z_{\lceil b_j \rceil}$ in proportion to their distance from $b_j$.

The use of a projection rather than direct Wasserstein minimization is motivated by a theoretical result (Proposition 5 in the paper): the **sample Wasserstein gradient is biased** — the gradient of the sample Wasserstein distance does not equal the expected gradient of the per-sample distances. This makes stochastic gradient descent on the Wasserstein loss unreliable. The categorical projection with cross-entropy loss avoids this problem entirely, and empirically minimizes the Wasserstein metric more effectively than direct Wasserstein minimization.
