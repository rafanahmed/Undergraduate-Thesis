The Wasserstein metric (also called the Kantorovich or Mallows metric) measures the distance between two probability distributions in a way that respects the *geometry* of the outcome space. Unlike KL divergence or total variation, which only care about how probability mass is redistributed, the Wasserstein metric also accounts for *how far* mass must be moved. This geometric sensitivity is precisely why it serves as the correct metric for analyzing value distributions: a return of 10 is "close" to a return of 11, and the metric captures this.

The paper uses $d_p$ as the primary analytical tool for establishing contraction results. The distributional Bellman operator $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in the maximal form of this metric (see [[2.1 - Maximal Wasserstein Metric (M. Bellemare et al., 2017)]]) — but notably *not* in total variation, KL divergence, or Kolmogorov distance.

$$
d_p(F, G) := \inf_{U, V} \|U - V\|_p = \left(\int_0^1 \left|F^{-1}(u) - G^{-1}(u)\right|^p du\right)^{1/p}
$$

Where,

| $d_p(F, G)$  | Wasserstein $p$-distance between cumulative distribution functions $F$ and $G$.                                                                      |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $F, G$        | Cumulative distribution functions over $\mathbb{R}$.                                                                                                 |
| $\inf_{U,V}$  | Infimum over all pairs of random variables $(U, V)$ whose CDFs are $F$ and $G$ respectively — i.e., over all couplings.                             |
| $\|U - V\|_p$ | $L_p$ norm of the difference $U - V$, equal to $\left(\mathbb{E}\left[\left|U - V\right|^p\right]\right)^{1/p}$.                                    |
| $F^{-1}(u)$   | Inverse CDF (quantile function) of $F$: $F^{-1}(u) := \inf\{y : F(y) \ge u\}$.                                                                     |
| $G^{-1}(u)$   | Inverse CDF of $G$.                                                                                                                                 |
| $u$            | Quantile level, integrated uniformly over $[0, 1]$.                                                                                                  |
| $p$            | Order of the metric, $1 \le p \le \infty$.                                                                                                           |

The infimum in the definition is attained by the **inverse CDF coupling**: setting $U = F^{-1}(W)$ and $V = G^{-1}(W)$ for $W \sim \text{Uniform}[0, 1]$, which yields the integral form on the right. This provides a constructive way to compute $d_p$: align both distributions by their quantile functions and measure the pointwise $L_p$ difference.

Three properties of $d_p$ are fundamental to the paper's contraction proofs:
- **(P1) Scalar scaling:** $d_p(aU, aV) \le |a|\,d_p(U, V)$ — used to extract the discount factor $\gamma$.
- **(P2) Shift invariance:** $d_p(A + U, A + V) \le d_p(U, V)$ for $A$ independent of $U, V$ — used to remove the shared reward $R(x, a)$.
- **(P3) Random scaling:** $d_p(AU, AV) \le \|A\|_p\,d_p(U, V)$ — used to handle random transitions.

These properties make $d_p$ the natural metric for distributional RL: the Bellman update involves exactly the operations (shifting by reward, scaling by $\gamma$, mixing over transitions) that $d_p$ handles cleanly.
