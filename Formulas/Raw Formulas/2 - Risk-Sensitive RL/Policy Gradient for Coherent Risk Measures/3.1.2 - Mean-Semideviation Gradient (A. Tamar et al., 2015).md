The mean-semideviation gradient is a special case of the static gradient formula (see [[3.1 - Static Gradient Formula (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) applied to the **mean-semideviation** risk measure $\rho_{\text{MSD}}(Z; \alpha) = \mathbb{E}[Z] + \alpha \cdot \text{SD}[Z]$, where $\text{SD}[Z] = (\mathbb{E}[(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z])^2_+])^{1/2}$ is the *semi-deviation* — the standard deviation of only the positive (above-mean) deviations.

Unlike standard deviation, the semi-deviation penalizes only *downside* variability (costs above the mean), making it a more rational risk measure for decision-makers who should not avert high returns. The mean-semideviation is coherent for $\alpha \in [0, 1]$. The gradient is derived in Proposition 4.3 / B.1 using the Envelope Theorem on the dual representation of the semi-deviation as a supremum over an $L_2$-ball.

$$
\nabla_\theta \rho_{\text{MSD}}(Z;\, \alpha) = \nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}[Z] + \frac{\alpha}{\text{SD}(Z)}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z])_+\!\left(\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)\,(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z]) - \nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}[Z]\right)\right]
$$

Where,

| $\nabla_\theta \rho_{\text{MSD}}(Z; \alpha)$          | Gradient of the mean-semideviation risk measure with respect to $\theta$.                                              |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}[Z]$                          | Gradient of the expected cost: $\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)\,Z]$ by the standard likelihood-ratio method.  |
| $\alpha$                                                 | Risk-aversion parameter, $\alpha \in [0, 1]$. Controls the weight placed on downside variability.                      |
| $\text{SD}(Z)$                                           | Semi-deviation: $(\mathbb{E}[(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z])^2_+])^{1/2}$. Measures variability only above the mean.              |
| $(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z])_+$                                 | Positive part of the deviation from the mean. Equals $Z - \mathbb{E}[Z]$ when $Z > \mathbb{E}[Z]$, and $0$ otherwise. |
| $\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)$                          | Likelihood ratio (score function) at outcome $\omega$.                                                                  |

The gradient decomposes cleanly: the **first term** is the standard policy gradient of the mean cost, and the **second term** is a correction that weights the policy gradient signal by how far above the mean each outcome falls, normalized by the semi-deviation. Only outcomes *above the mean* contribute to the correction — outcomes below the mean are zeroed out by the $(Z - \mathbb{E}[Z])_+$ factor.

This asymmetric treatment of upside vs. downside variability is what makes the mean-semideviation superior to the mean-standard-deviation in the paper's numerical experiment (Section 6). When applied to an asset with a heavy upper tail (Pareto distribution, $\alpha = 1.5$), the semideviation correctly identifies it as low-risk (the heavy tail is on the *upside*), while the standard deviation incorrectly penalizes the high returns and drives the agent toward an inferior, stochastically dominated asset.

The gradient formula can be estimated by replacing all expectations with sample averages, yielding the **GMSD algorithm** (Algorithm 1 in Appendix B).
