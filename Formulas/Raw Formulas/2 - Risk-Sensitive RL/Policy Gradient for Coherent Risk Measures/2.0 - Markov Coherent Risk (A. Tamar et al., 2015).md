The Markov coherent risk measure extends the static coherent risk framework to sequential decision problems by applying risk evaluation *recursively* at each stage of an MDP trajectory. This recursive nesting — rather than applying risk to the total return as a lump sum — is what guarantees **time consistency**: if a policy is risk-optimal for an $n$-stage problem, the sub-policy from any intermediate stage onward is also risk-optimal. Time consistency is essential for the Bellman equation to hold, enabling dynamic programming.

The measure was introduced by Ruszczyński (2010) and is central to the paper's treatment of the **Dynamic Risk Problem (DRP)**. At each state $x$, the static coherent risk $\rho$ is *induced by* the transition probability $P_\theta(\cdot|x)$, making the risk evaluation Markov — it depends only on the current state, not the full history.

$$
\rho_T(M) = C(x_0) + \gamma\rho\!\left(C(x_1) + \cdots + \gamma\rho\!\left(C(x_{T-1}) + \gamma\rho(C(x_T))\right)\right)
$$

Where,

| $\rho_T(M)$       | Markov coherent risk of the MDP $M$ over a $T$-length horizon.                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $M$                | Markov Decision Process $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A}, C, P, \gamma, x_0)$.                                                                                                                   |
| $C(x_t)$          | Bounded deterministic cost at state $x_t$, with $C(x) \in [-C_{\max}, C_{\max}]$.                                                                                                          |
| $\gamma$           | Discount factor, $\gamma \in (0, 1)$.                                                                                                                                                      |
| $\rho$             | Static coherent risk measure satisfying the four axioms and Assumption 2.2. At each state $x$, it is induced by $P_\theta(\cdot\|x) = \sum_a P(x'\|x,a)\mu_\theta(a\|x)$.                  |
| $x_0, \ldots, x_T$ | Trajectory of states drawn from MDP $M$ under policy $\mu_\theta$.                                                                                                                        |

The **recursive nesting** is the defining feature: risk is not evaluated on the cumulative return as a single lump sum (that would be the *static* risk problem), but is applied *stage by stage*, each time wrapping the future risk inside the current-stage evaluation. This structure is precisely what enables the risk-sensitive Bellman equation (see [[4.0 - Risk-Sensitive Bellman Equation (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).

The infinite-horizon version $\rho_\infty(M) := \lim_{T \to \infty} \rho_T(M)$ is well-defined since $\gamma < 1$ and costs are bounded. The paper's dynamic optimization target is $\min_\theta \rho_\infty(M)$, and the gradient $\nabla_\theta \rho_\infty(M) = \nabla_\theta V_\theta(x_0)$ is derived via the Dynamic Policy Gradient Theorem (see [[4.1.1 - Dynamic Policy Gradient Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).

The **Markov property** of this risk measure means that each static risk evaluation at state $x$ depends only on the transition probabilities from $x$, not on the trajectory history. Without this property, the risk evaluation would require tracking the entire past, destroying the computational tractability that dynamic programming provides.
