The Dynamic Policy Gradient Theorem (Theorem 5.2 / E.4) is the paper's second central result and its most important contribution. It generalizes the standard Policy Gradient Theorem (Sutton et al., 2000) from expected returns to **Markov coherent dynamic risk measures**, providing the theoretical foundation for risk-sensitive actor-critic algorithms. The gradient of the risk-sensitive value function is expressed as an expectation over trajectories sampled under $\xi^*$-weighted transitions — the worst-case MDP induced by the risk envelope.

The theorem combines the static gradient formula's Envelope Theorem approach (see [[3.1 - Static Gradient Formula (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) with the risk-sensitive Bellman equation's recursive structure (see [[4.0 - Risk-Sensitive Bellman Equation (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]). The stage-wise cost function $h_\theta$ (see [[4.1 - Stage-wise Cost Function (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) replaces the standard $Q$-function.

$$
\nabla V_\theta(x) = \mathbb{E}_{\xi^*_\theta}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t\,\nabla_\theta \log \mu_\theta(a_t | x_t)\,h_\theta(x_t, a_t) \;\middle|\; x_0 = x\right]
$$

Where,

| $\nabla V_\theta(x)$                      | Gradient of the risk-sensitive value function at state $x$ with respect to $\theta$. Since $V_\theta(x_0) = \rho_\infty(M)$, this gives $\nabla_\theta \rho_\infty(M)$. |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| $\mathbb{E}_{\xi^*_\theta}[\cdot]$        | Expectation over trajectories generated by $\xi^*$-weighted transitions: $P_\theta(\cdot\|x)\xi^*_{\theta,x}(\cdot)$.                                                   |
| $\gamma^t$                                 | Discount factor raised to the $t$-th power. Weights future contributions geometrically.                                                                                   |
| $\nabla_\theta \log \mu_\theta(a_t\|x_t)$ | Likelihood ratio (score function) of the policy at time $t$. Well-defined and bounded by Assumption 5.1.                                                                 |
| $h_\theta(x_t, a_t)$                      | [[4.1 - Stage-wise Cost Function (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]. The risk-sensitive analogue of the action-value function.                                                     |
| $x_0 = x$                                  | Initial state conditioning.                                                                                                                                               |
| $\mu_\theta(a\|x)$                        | Parameterized stationary Markov policy.                                                                                                                                   |

The theorem has the same form as the standard Policy Gradient Theorem — a discounted sum of score functions times cost signals — but with two critical differences. First, **trajectories are sampled under $\xi^*$-weighted transitions** $P_\theta(\cdot|x)\xi^*_{\theta,x}(\cdot)$ rather than the original transitions $P_\theta(\cdot|x)$. The agent effectively simulates the worst-case MDP corresponding to the risk envelope. Second, **the cost signal is $h_\theta$** rather than the standard $Q$-function, incorporating KKT multiplier corrections from the risk envelope constraints.

In the **risk-neutral case** ($\xi^* = 1$ everywhere, no constraint terms), the theorem reduces to the familiar formula $\nabla V(x) = \mathbb{E}[\sum_t \gamma^t \nabla \log \mu(a_t|x_t)\,Q(x_t, a_t)]$.

The proof (Appendix D) applies the Envelope Theorem to the risk-sensitive Bellman equation at each state, defines $\hat{h}_\theta(x) = \sum_a \mu_\theta(a|x)\nabla_\theta \log \mu_\theta(a|x)\,h_\theta(x, a)$, and unfolds the resulting recursion. The infinite sum converges by bounded convergence since $\gamma^t \nabla V_\theta(x) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$.

Under function approximation $V_\theta(x) \approx v^{*\top}_\theta \phi(x)$, the gradient estimate converges to $\nabla V_\theta(x_0)$ up to the approximation error $\Delta = \|\Phi v^*_\theta - V_\theta\|_\infty$ (Theorem E.5). This enables a practical **actor-critic algorithm**: the critic approximates $V_\theta$ via PRSVI, the actor estimates $\nabla V_\theta$ via the two-phase sampling procedure derived from this theorem.
