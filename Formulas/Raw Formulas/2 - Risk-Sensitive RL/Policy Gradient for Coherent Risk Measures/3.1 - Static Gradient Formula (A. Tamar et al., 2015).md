The static gradient formula (Theorem 4.2) is the paper's first central result. It provides a sampling-based expression for $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z)$ — the gradient of any coherent risk measure with respect to the policy parameter $\theta$. The formula is derived by applying the **Envelope Theorem** for saddle-point problems to the risk envelope Lagrangian (see [[3.0 - Risk Envelope Lagrangian (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) and then converting the resulting sum into an expectation via the **likelihood-ratio trick**.

The formula generalizes previously known results: the CVaR gradient (see [[3.1.1 - CVaR Gradient (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) and the mean-semideviation gradient (see [[3.1.2 - Mean-Semideviation Gradient (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) are both special cases. It also serves as a building block for the dynamic policy gradient theorem (see [[4.1.1 - Dynamic Policy Gradient Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).

$$
\nabla_\theta \rho(Z) = \mathbb{E}_{\xi^*_\theta}\!\left[\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)\,(Z - \lambda^{*,P}_\theta)\right] - \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}} \lambda^{*,E}_\theta(e)\,\nabla_\theta g_e(\xi^*_\theta;\, P_\theta) - \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \lambda^{*,I}_\theta(i)\,\nabla_\theta f_i(\xi^*_\theta;\, P_\theta)
$$

Where,

| $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z)$                | Gradient of the coherent risk measure with respect to the policy parameter $\theta$.                                                                                       |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\mathbb{E}_{\xi^*_\theta}[\cdot]$     | Expectation under the adversarial density $\xi^*_\theta$ — the worst-case density from the risk envelope that attains $\rho(Z) = \mathbb{E}_{\xi^*_\theta}[Z]$.           |
| $\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)$          | Likelihood ratio (score function). Well-defined and bounded by Assumption 4.1.                                                                                             |
| $Z$                                     | Random cost variable.                                                                                                                                                      |
| $\lambda^{*,P}_\theta$                  | KKT multiplier for the probability normalization constraint. Acts as an automatic **baseline** that reduces variance in the gradient estimate.                             |
| $\xi^*_\theta$                          | Optimal test density from the saddle point of the Lagrangian (see [[3.0 - Risk Envelope Lagrangian (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).                                             |
| $\lambda^{*,E}_\theta(e)$              | KKT multipliers for equality constraints $g_e$, at the saddle point.                                                                                                       |
| $\nabla_\theta g_e(\xi^*_\theta; P_\theta)$ | Gradient of the equality constraint functions with respect to $\theta$, evaluated at the saddle point.                                                                |
| $\lambda^{*,I}_\theta(i)$              | KKT multipliers for inequality constraints $f_i$, at the saddle point.                                                                                                     |
| $\nabla_\theta f_i(\xi^*_\theta; P_\theta)$ | Gradient of the inequality constraint functions with respect to $\theta$, evaluated at the saddle point.                                                              |

The formula decomposes into two components. The **first term** has the classic REINFORCE structure — $\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega) \cdot (\text{cost signal})$ — but under the adversarial density $\xi^*_\theta$ rather than the base distribution. The multiplier $\lambda^{*,P}_\theta$ arises naturally from the KKT conditions and serves as a variance-reducing baseline. The **second and third terms** are constraint correction terms that account for the $\theta$-dependence of the risk envelope constraints; they vanish entirely for risk measures whose envelopes do not depend on $\theta$ (e.g., CVaR).

For risk measures with **closed-form saddle points** (CVaR, mean-semideviation), the formula can be evaluated directly from samples. For general coherent risk, a **Sample Average Approximation (SAA)** procedure solves the empirical Lagrangian via convex programming to obtain $(\xi^*_{\theta;N}, \lambda^{*,P}_{\theta;N}, \lambda^{*,E}_{\theta;N}, \lambda^{*,I}_{\theta;N})$, then plugs these into the formula. Proposition 4.4 establishes that this SAA estimator is consistent: it converges to $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z)$ with probability 1 as $N \to \infty$.
