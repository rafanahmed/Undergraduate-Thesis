The risk envelope gives the **canonical convex programming form** for the constraint set $\mathcal{U}(P_\theta)$ that appears in the Representation Theorem (see [[1.0 - Representation Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]). Every coherent risk measure is uniquely identified by its risk envelope, and the envelope's structure — affine equality constraints, convex inequality constraints, and Slater's condition — is what makes the Lagrangian well-behaved and the Envelope Theorem applicable.

This formulation is codified as Assumption 2.2 in the paper. The smoothness and boundedness conditions on the constraint functions $g_e$ and $f_i$ ensure that the gradient formula of Theorem 4.2 involves well-defined derivatives. These conditions hold for all commonly used coherent risk measures including CVaR, mean-semideviation, and spectral risk measures.

$$
\mathcal{U}(P_\theta) = \left\{\xi P_\theta \,:\, g_e(\xi, P_\theta) = 0 \;\forall e \in \mathcal{E},\; f_i(\xi, P_\theta) \leq 0 \;\forall i \in \mathcal{I},\; \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} \xi(\omega)P_\theta(\omega) = 1,\; \xi(\omega) \geq 0 \right\}
$$

Where,

| $\mathcal{U}(P_\theta)$ | Risk envelope. The convex, bounded, closed set of feasible test densities that defines a particular coherent risk measure (see [[1.0 - Representation Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]). |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\xi$                    | Test density function. Re-weights the base probability $P_\theta$.                                                                                                                       |
| $P_\theta$               | Base probability measure parameterized by $\theta$.                                                                                                                                      |
| $g_e(\xi, P_\theta)$    | Equality constraint functions, **affine** in $\xi$. Indexed by $e \in \mathcal{E}$.                                                                                                     |
| $\mathcal{E}$            | Index set for equality constraints.                                                                                                                                                      |
| $f_i(\xi, P_\theta)$    | Inequality constraint functions, **convex** in $\xi$. Indexed by $i \in \mathcal{I}$.                                                                                                   |
| $\mathcal{I}$            | Index set for inequality constraints.                                                                                                                                                    |
| $\sum_\omega \xi(\omega)P_\theta(\omega) = 1$ | Probability normalization constraint. Ensures $\xi P_\theta$ is a valid probability distribution.                                                                |
| $\xi(\omega) \geq 0$    | Non-negativity constraint on the density function.                                                                                                                                       |

The risk envelope's **convex programming structure** is essential for two reasons. First, Slater's condition (existence of a strictly feasible $\xi$) guarantees **strong duality** for the Lagrangian of the Representation Theorem optimization, which is required for the Envelope Theorem to apply (see [[3.0 - Risk Envelope Lagrangian (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]). Second, the smoothness of $f_i(\xi, p)$ and $g_e(\xi, p)$ in $p$ — with uniformly bounded derivatives $|df_i/dp(\omega)| \leq M$ and $|dg_e/dp(\omega)| \leq M$ — ensures the constraint correction terms in the gradient formula are well-defined.

Different risk measures instantiate different envelopes: CVaR at level $\alpha$ uses $\mathcal{U} = \{\xi P_\theta : \xi(\omega) \in [0, \alpha^{-1}]\}$ with no equality constraints; mean-semideviation uses an $L_2$-ball constraint on $\xi$; the worst-case risk uses $\mathcal{U} = \mathcal{B}$ (the full simplex). In each case, the constraints determine which density functions the adversary can choose, and thus which aspects of the cost distribution the risk measure emphasizes.
