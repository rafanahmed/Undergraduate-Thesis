The CVaR gradient is a special case of the static gradient formula (see [[3.1 - Static Gradient Formula (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) applied to the **Conditional Value at Risk** at level $\alpha \in [0, 1]$. CVaR is one of the most widely used coherent risk measures in finance and operations research. For continuous distributions, CVaR at level $\alpha$ equals the mean of the $\alpha$-tail: $\mathbb{E}[Z \mid Z > q_\alpha]$, where $q_\alpha$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile.

The CVaR risk envelope is $\mathcal{U} = \{\xi P_\theta : \xi(\omega) \in [0, \alpha^{-1}], \sum_\omega \xi(\omega)P_\theta(\omega) = 1\}$, with no $\theta$-dependent constraints. The saddle point satisfies $\xi^*(\omega) = \alpha^{-1}$ when $Z(\omega) > q_\alpha$ and $\xi^*(\omega) = 0$ when $Z(\omega) < q_\alpha$. Since the constraint correction terms vanish (no $\theta$-dependence in the envelope), only the first term of the general formula survives.

$$
\nabla_\theta \rho_{\text{CVaR}}(Z;\, \alpha) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)\,(Z - q_\alpha) \;\middle|\; Z(\omega) > q_\alpha\right]
$$

Where,

| $\nabla_\theta \rho_{\text{CVaR}}(Z; \alpha)$ | Gradient of CVaR at level $\alpha$ with respect to $\theta$.                                                              |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\nabla_\theta \log P(\omega)$                  | Likelihood ratio (score function) at outcome $\omega$.                                                                    |
| $Z$                                              | Random cost variable.                                                                                                     |
| $q_\alpha$                                       | The $(1 - \alpha)$-quantile of $Z$. Serves as the natural baseline in the conditional expectation.                        |
| $\alpha$                                         | CVaR level parameter, $\alpha \in [0, 1]$. Smaller $\alpha$ makes CVaR more sensitive to rare, extreme costs.             |
| $Z(\omega) > q_\alpha$                           | Conditioning event: only outcomes in the upper $\alpha$-tail of the cost distribution contribute to the gradient.         |

The formula says: the gradient of CVaR is a standard policy gradient computed *only over the tail* of the cost distribution, with the quantile $q_\alpha$ as a natural baseline. This re-derives the result of Tamar et al. (2015) as a special case of Theorem 4.2, but under weaker assumptions and with a considerably simpler proof.

The **practical implication** is that CVaR gradient estimation inherently suffers from *sample inefficiency*: only the fraction $\alpha$ of samples that fall in the tail contribute to the gradient, while the remaining $1 - \alpha$ fraction is discarded. For small $\alpha$ (high tail sensitivity), this makes convergence slow â€” a fundamental challenge that the paper identifies as motivating future work on importance sampling.
