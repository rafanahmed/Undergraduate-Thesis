The Representation Theorem is the foundational result that makes the entire paper possible. It establishes that any **coherent risk measure** — one satisfying the four axioms of convexity, monotonicity, translation invariance, and positive homogeneity — can be expressed as a *worst-case expectation* over a convex set of probability density functions. This converts a potentially opaque risk functional into a tractable optimization: maximizing a linear objective over a convex constraint set, which is the structure needed for gradient-based optimization.

The theorem originates from Artzner et al. (1999) and Shapiro et al. (2009). Different coherent risk measures correspond to different **risk envelopes** $\mathcal{U}$: the expectation uses a singleton envelope, CVaR uses densities bounded by $\alpha^{-1}$, and the worst-case risk uses the entire probability simplex. The risk envelope uniquely identifies the risk measure and vice versa.

$$
\rho(Z) = \max_{\xi \,:\, \xi P_\theta \in \mathcal{U}(P_\theta)} \mathbb{E}_\xi[Z] = \max_{\xi \,:\, \xi P_\theta \in \mathcal{U}(P_\theta)} \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} P_\theta(\omega)\,\xi(\omega)\,Z(\omega)
$$

Where,

| $\rho(Z)$          | Coherent risk measure applied to the random cost $Z$. Maps an uncertain outcome to a scalar risk value.                                                                             |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| $Z$                 | Random variable representing a cost, defined on the sample space $\Omega$. Interpreted as a cost: lower is better.                                                                   |
| $\xi$               | Test density function (Radon-Nikodym derivative). Re-weights the base probability $P_\theta$; when $\xi(\omega) = 1$ everywhere, the $\xi$-weighted expectation equals the standard expectation. |
| $P_\theta(\omega)$  | Base probability of outcome $\omega \in \Omega$, parameterized by policy parameter $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^K$.                                                                       |
| $\mathcal{U}(P_\theta)$ | **Risk envelope**. A convex, bounded, closed set of test densities that uniquely characterizes the coherent risk measure (see [[1.1 - Risk Envelope (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).  |
| $\mathbb{E}_\xi[Z]$ | $\xi$-weighted expectation of $Z$: $\sum_{\omega} P_\theta(\omega)\xi(\omega)Z(\omega)$.                                                                                           |
| $\Omega$            | Finite sample space (the set of all possible outcomes).                                                                                                                              |
| $\max$              | Maximization over all feasible density functions $\xi$ such that $\xi P_\theta \in \mathcal{U}(P_\theta)$.                                                                          |

The Representation Theorem is the **linchpin** of the paper's gradient derivation strategy. Because the risk is expressed as the optimal value of a convex program (maximizing a linear objective over a convex set), the **Envelope Theorem** can be applied to differentiate through the optimization, yielding the gradient $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z)$ without needing to differentiate the risk functional directly. The adversarial density $\xi^*$ that attains the maximum plays a central role: the gradient of the risk becomes a policy gradient *under* $\xi^*$, which is the key insight behind the static gradient formula (see [[3.1 - Static Gradient Formula (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).

The max-of-expectations structure also establishes the duality between **coherent risk** and **robust optimization**: optimizing a coherent risk measure is equivalent to optimizing expected cost under the worst-case probability model within the envelope. This connects risk-sensitive RL to robust MDPs (Osogami, 2012) and underlies the paper's use of robust TD algorithms for value function approximation.
