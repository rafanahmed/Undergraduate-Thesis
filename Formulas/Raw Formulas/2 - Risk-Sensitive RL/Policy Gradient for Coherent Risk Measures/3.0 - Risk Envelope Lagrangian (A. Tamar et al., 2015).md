The Lagrangian encodes the constrained optimization problem from the Representation Theorem (see [[1.0 - Representation Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]) with the risk envelope's constraint structure (see [[1.1 - Risk Envelope (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]). It is the intermediate object through which the **Envelope Theorem** is applied to derive the gradient $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z)$.

Slater's condition (from Assumption 2.2) guarantees **strong duality**: the max-min and min-max of the Lagrangian coincide, and the set of saddle points $\mathcal{S}$ is non-empty and bounded. This strong duality is the prerequisite for the Envelope Theorem application — without it, the gradient of the optimal value would not equal the gradient of the Lagrangian at the saddle point.

$$
L_\theta(\xi, \lambda_P, \lambda_E, \lambda_I) = \sum_{\omega \in \Omega} \xi(\omega)\,P_\theta(\omega)\,Z(\omega) - \lambda_P\!\left(\sum_{\omega \in \Omega} \xi(\omega)\,P_\theta(\omega) - 1\right) - \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}} \lambda_E(e)\,g_e(\xi, P_\theta) - \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \lambda_I(i)\,f_i(\xi, P_\theta)
$$

Where,

| $L_\theta$         | Lagrangian function of the risk envelope optimization, parameterized by $\theta$.                                                                   |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\xi(\omega)$       | Test density at outcome $\omega$. The primal variable being optimized (see [[1.0 - Representation Theorem (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).               |
| $P_\theta(\omega)$  | Base probability of outcome $\omega$, parameterized by $\theta$.                                                                                    |
| $Z(\omega)$         | Cost realization at outcome $\omega$.                                                                                                               |
| $\lambda_P$         | Lagrange multiplier for the probability normalization constraint $\sum_\omega \xi(\omega)P_\theta(\omega) = 1$. Serves as an automatic baseline in the gradient formula. |
| $\lambda_E(e)$      | Lagrange multipliers for equality constraints $g_e(\xi, P_\theta) = 0$, indexed by $e \in \mathcal{E}$.                                            |
| $g_e(\xi, P_\theta)$ | Affine equality constraints from the risk envelope (see [[1.1 - Risk Envelope (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).                                         |
| $\lambda_I(i)$      | Lagrange multipliers for inequality constraints $f_i(\xi, P_\theta) \leq 0$, indexed by $i \in \mathcal{I}$. Non-negative: $\lambda_I(i) \geq 0$. |
| $f_i(\xi, P_\theta)$ | Convex inequality constraints from the risk envelope (see [[1.1 - Risk Envelope (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).                                       |

The Lagrangian's saddle-point structure — convex in $\xi$, concave in $(\lambda_P, \lambda_E, \lambda_I)$ — ensures strong duality: $\rho(Z) = \max_{\xi \geq 0} \min_{\lambda_P, \lambda_I \geq 0, \lambda_E} L_\theta = \min_{\lambda_P, \lambda_I \geq 0, \lambda_E} \max_{\xi \geq 0} L_\theta$. The Envelope Theorem (Milgrom & Segal, 2002) then gives $\nabla_\theta \rho(Z) = \nabla_\theta L_\theta |_{(\xi^*_\theta, \lambda^{*,P}_\theta, \lambda^{*,E}_\theta, \lambda^{*,I}_\theta)}$, which is computed explicitly to produce the static gradient formula (see [[3.1 - Static Gradient Formula (A. Tamar et al., 2015)]]).

The **KKT multipliers** $(\lambda^{*,P}_\theta, \lambda^{*,E}_\theta, \lambda^{*,I}_\theta)$ at the saddle point play dual roles: they enforce optimality in the Lagrangian, and they appear directly in the gradient formula as the baseline ($\lambda^{*,P}_\theta$) and constraint correction terms ($\lambda^{*,E}_\theta$, $\lambda^{*,I}_\theta$). For risk measures like CVaR, these multipliers have closed-form expressions; for general coherent risk, they are obtained from a convex programming solver.
