The deterministic policy gradient theorem establishes the gradient for optimizing a *deterministic* policy $\mu_\theta(s)$ in continuous action spaces. Unlike the stochastic policy gradient which requires sampling actions and computing log-probabilities, the deterministic variant directly differentiates through the policy and the Q-function using the chain rule. This is the theoretical basis of **DDPG**, one of the baseline methods evaluated in the paper.

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\mu_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_s\left[\nabla_\theta \mu_\theta(s)\, \nabla_a Q^{\mu_\theta}(s, a)\big|_{a = \mu_\theta(s)}\right]
$$

Where,

| $\nabla_\theta J(\mu_\theta)$                              | Gradient of the expected policy value with respect to $\theta$ for the deterministic policy.          |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\mu_\theta(s)$                                             | Deterministic policy. Maps state $s$ directly to a continuous action $a = \mu_\theta(s)$.             |
| $\nabla_\theta \mu_\theta(s)$                               | Jacobian of the policy output with respect to the parameters $\theta$.                                |
| $\nabla_a Q^{\mu_\theta}(s, a)\big|_{a = \mu_\theta(s)}$   | Gradient of the Q-function with respect to the action, evaluated at the policy's output.              |
| $Q^{\mu_\theta}(s, a)$                                      | Action-value function under the deterministic policy $\mu_\theta$.                                    |
| $\mathbb{E}_s$                                              | Expectation over the state distribution induced by the policy $\mu_\theta$.                           |

The deterministic policy gradient avoids the need to integrate over the action space (as required by stochastic methods), making it computationally attractive for continuous control. However, in the context of parameterized action spaces, DDPG-based approaches suffer from two problems identified in the paper: (1) **continuous relaxation** of the discrete action component flattens the hierarchical structure and may increase action space complexity, and (2) empirically, DDPG exhibits extreme variance and often fails to learn reasonable policies in parameterized environments — an instability confirmed by both Hausknecht and Stone (2016) and Wei et al. (2018). The paper's H-PPO architecture avoids these issues by using *stochastic* policies optimized with PPO's clipped objective.
