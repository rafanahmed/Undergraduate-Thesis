The Bellman equation provides the recursive characterization of the action-value function $Q(s, a)$ in discrete action spaces. It decomposes the value at any state-action pair into the *immediate expected reward* plus the *discounted expected optimal future value* under the environment's transition dynamics. This equation is the backbone of Q-learning and DQN, and serves as the value-based foundation against which the paper's policy-gradient approach is contrasted.

$$
Q(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_{r_t, s_{t+1}}\left[r_t + \gamma \max_{a' \in A} Q(s_{t+1}, a') \;\middle|\; s_t = s,\, a_t = a\right]
$$

Where,

| $Q(s, a)$        | Action-value function. Expected cumulative discounted reward from taking action $a$ in state $s$ and following the optimal policy thereafter. |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $s$               | Current state observed by the agent.                                                                                                          |
| $a$               | Action taken by the agent from the action space $A$.                                                                                          |
| $r_t$             | Immediate reward received at timestep $t$.                                                                                                    |
| $\gamma$          | Discount factor, $\gamma \in [0, 1)$. Controls the trade-off between immediate and future rewards.                                            |
| $\max_{a' \in A}$ | Maximization over all actions in the action space $A$ at the next state $s_{t+1}$.                                                            |
| $s_{t+1}$         | Next state, sampled from the environment's transition dynamics given $(s_t, a_t)$.                                                            |
| $\mathbb{E}$      | Expectation over the stochastic reward $r_t$ and the stochastic transition to $s_{t+1}$.                                                     |

The Bellman equation is the foundation of **value-based RL** in discrete action spaces. DQN approximates $Q$ with a deep neural network and uses this recursion as the training target. In the context of this paper, the Bellman equation represents the standard discrete-action paradigm that parameterized action methods must move beyond — it assumes the action space $A$ is a flat finite set, with no internal structure. The paper's critique is that directly applying this framework to parameterized spaces (via discretization of continuous parameters) produces exponentially large action sets that destroy the method's tractability.
